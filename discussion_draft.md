# 6. Discussion

## 6.1 Orientation and headline reading

Chapter 5 reported the empirical outcome of the comparison. This chapter does not revisit those results. Instead, it asks what they mean: for the conditions under which they were produced, for the broader applicability of heterogeneous graph methods to financial crime, and for the regulatory and operational settings in which any such system would have to live.

The chapter is organised in two halves. Section 6.2 interprets the findings of Chapter 5 directly, examining the feature asymmetry built into the experimental design, the temporal generalisation gap that the GNN architectures exhibit to differing degrees, and the divergence between metric performance and operational cost performance that emerges most clearly in the HeteroGAT result. Sections 6.3 through 6.5 broaden the discussion outward, situating the findings within the open problems Cheng et al. (2024) catalogue for graph-based fraud detection — heterogeneous structure, temporal dynamics, label scarcity, scalability to real-time streams, and interpretability under KYC regulation — each of which surfaces in the present study. Section 6.6 then makes explicit the structural limitations under which the empirical claim holds, and Section 6.7 sets out the directions in which the work most naturally extends.

## 6.2 Interpreting the findings

The interpretation of Chapter 5's results turns on three observations that the headline metrics alone do not surface. The first is that the experimental design embeds a deliberate asymmetry in the features available to each track, and the asymmetry rather than a like-for-like comparison is what gives the research question its substantive content. The second is that the validation-to-test PR-AUC drops vary widely across the three GNN architectures, and this variation is itself diagnostic of how each architecture handles temporal distributional shift. The third is that the model with the lowest test PR-AUC by a non-trivial margin — HeteroGAT — produces the lowest fraud-lost figure of any model at any threshold considered, which is incompatible with the simple reading that HeteroGAT is uniformly weaker than the tabular baseline. The three subsections below take each in turn.

### 6.2.1 Feature asymmetry

The XGBoost baseline and the heterogeneous GNNs were not given the same inputs. The XGBoost baseline received the full engineered feature set, including velocity aggregations such as `cust_paymentsub_sum_7D`, `cust_amt_sum_7D`, and `cust_amt_sum_30D`, customer-level novelty flags such as `is_new_intl_dest_for_customer`, behavioural anomaly indicators such as `txn_to_median_ratio`, and target-encoded counterparty identifiers. The heterogeneous GNNs received only the lean per-edge feature set of thirty raw transaction attributes — log amount, channel, clearing system, currency, destination, international flag, and cyclical time encoding — without any of the customer-level aggregations that dominate the XGBoost feature importance ranking reported in Section 5.1.4.

This asymmetry is methodologically deliberate. The research question asks whether graph structure carries signal that improves fraud detection beyond what tabular features alone can provide; the corresponding experimental design therefore deliberately withholds from the GNN track the very features that would make a parity comparison mechanical. Message passing over the heterogeneous graph is, in effect, an architectural alternative to feature engineering — an attempt to recover from the local neighbourhood the customer-level behavioural context that velocity aggregations capture explicitly (Hamilton, 2020; Cheng et al., 2024). The comparison is therefore not between "the same model with and without features" but between two different ways of bringing relational context to bear on a transaction-level decision: explicit pre-computation in the tabular case, learned aggregation in the graph case.

The implication for how the headline result should be read is meaningful. The XGBoost feature importance analysis in Section 5.1.4 shows that velocity features and counterparty target encodings together account for the majority of the model's gain — exactly the customer- and counterparty-level relational signal that the graph-based track was designed to recover structurally. That XGBoost outperforms the GNNs on PR-AUC under these conditions is not, on its own, evidence that graph structure is uninformative; it is evidence that a two-layer heterogeneous GNN operating only on raw edge features and a graph topology consisting of two node types and two edge types, with the receptive field restricted to immediate counterparties, does not in this specific configuration recover as much relational signal as Danske Bank's existing feature engineering pipeline embeds in the tabular feature set. This is a narrower claim than the headline number alone suggests.

The narrower claim is worth holding onto because it points to a specific direction of attack rather than a general failure of graph methods on fraud data. Two changes to the experimental design would be required to settle the broader question. The first is to give the GNNs access to the same velocity and novelty features, either as additional edge features or as customer-node features in an extended graph topology. The second is to compare both tracks on the lean feature set alone, removing the velocity features from XGBoost and forcing both architectures to extract relational context from raw transaction attributes only. The expected outcomes of these two complementary studies are not symmetric, and the asymmetry is itself the substantive finding that the present design begins to expose: the value of graph structure is conditional on what alternative representations of customer-level context are already in hand. Section 6.7 returns to this as a candidate evaluation extension.

### 6.2.2 What the temporal generalisation gap reveals

The temporal generalisation gap reported in Section 5.3 is the central architectural finding of the comparison. Read at face value, the relative validation-to-test PR-AUC drops — XGBoost at -21%, HeteroGAT at -35%, HMPNN at -45%, HGT at -78% — show that all four models lose performance moving from the September-November 2025 validation window to the December 2025-March 2026 test window, but that the magnitude of the loss is heavily architecture-dependent. The XGBoost drop is the smallest in absolute and relative terms; the GNN drops are larger, with HGT collapsing to near-trivial test performance despite reasonable validation behaviour. The shape of this pattern is structurally informative.

Three architectural observations follow from the ordering. First, HGT's near-collapse on the test set is not consistent with the training dynamics observed during fitting. HGT achieved its best validation PR-AUC at epoch 60 and was early-stopped at epoch 135, a configuration that for any production model would be considered well-converged. The collapse therefore reflects a failure of generalisation rather than a failure of training, and the most plausible source is the architecture's heavy reliance on type-conditioned attention weights that are learned over the specific set of internal-account and external-account nodes seen during training. When the test window introduces counterparties that did not appear in training, HGT has no robust mechanism for generalising the type-attention pattern to them, and the model degrades sharply. The XGBoost target-encoded counterparty feature (`counterpartyid_te`) faces the same problem in principle — novel counterparties have no encoding — but XGBoost's reliance on velocity features and other transaction-level signals provides a fallback that HGT does not have. The result is that the most architecturally elegant of the three GNNs, the one that most fully embraces the type-aware attention paradigm of Hu et al. (2020), is also the most exposed to counterparty turnover.

Second, HeteroGAT's relatively shallow drop (-35%) compared to HGT and HMPNN suggests that edge-feature-conditioned attention generalises better than either pure type attention or edge-conditioned message functions. HeteroGAT projects edge features and adds them to attention logits before softmax, so the attention weights at inference time depend not only on the pair of node types and the learned per-relation parameters but also on the specific edge feature vector being scored. This makes the model's behaviour on novel edges depend less on having seen the specific counterparty during training, because the edge feature contribution to attention is well-defined for any edge regardless of whether its endpoints were observed at training time. The mechanism is similar in spirit to why XGBoost generalises well under counterparty turnover: both rely on per-transaction attributes that are stable across counterparty churn, supplemented by relational context only as a secondary signal. Veličković et al.'s (2018) original GAT formulation emphasised exactly this property — attention weights conditioned on input features rather than on identity — as the source of the architecture's robustness, and the present results suggest that the property survives the heterogeneous extension.

Third, HMPNN's intermediate drop (-45%) and its still-improving training trajectory at epoch 200 suggest a different failure mode. The NNConv layer that defines HMPNN parameterises its message function through a neural network that takes edge features as input and produces a message-weight matrix as output. This is the most expressive of the three architectures in terms of how edge features participate in message passing, but the expressiveness comes at a cost: the message-weight network has to be learned from data, and with 166,433 parameters trained against a long-tailed edge feature distribution and an extreme class imbalance, the learning is slow. The fact that HMPNN had not converged at epoch 200 — with validation PR-AUC of 0.34 still rising — is consistent with under-training rather than overfitting, and the relatively shallow test drop suggests that what HMPNN had learned by the early-stopping point generalised reasonably, but that the model would have continued to improve had compute permitted longer training. This is one of the places where the computational constraint discussed in Section 6.6.1 actively bounds what can be claimed from the result. Johannessen and Jullum (2023), who introduce HMPNN in the AML context, do not report explicit convergence diagnostics for their DNB experiments, but their reported final PR-AUC suggests that they trained to convergence on a substantially larger compute budget than was available here.

Taken together, the per-architecture drops point to a more general property of heterogeneous graph fraud detectors: their robustness to temporal distributional shift depends meaningfully on how counterparty-specific the learned representations are. Architectures that condition heavily on type-specific learned parameters (HGT) are most exposed; architectures whose attention is conditioned on per-edge features (HeteroGAT) are least exposed; architectures whose message function is itself a learned transformation of edge features (HMPNN) sit in between, with their robustness depending on how well the message function has been trained. This is a finding that survives the fact that XGBoost outperforms all three on the headline metric, and it is the kind of architectural diagnostic that a controlled cross-architecture comparison is uniquely positioned to surface.

> **Analytical gap to address before this section can stand:** the architectural interpretation above rests on an empirical claim about counterparty turnover between the training and test windows that is not yet substantiated. The required figures would be: (i) the proportion of test-window transactions whose counterparty was unseen during training, broken out by edge type; (ii) per-counterparty-coverage performance — i.e. PR-AUC restricted to test transactions whose counterparty appeared in training, versus PR-AUC on test transactions whose counterparty did not. If counterparty coverage in the test window is substantially lower than in the validation window, this directly explains the val→test drops; if it is similar, the architectural interpretation needs revisiting.

> **Analytical gap to address before this section can stand:** the section would also benefit from per-feature drift statistics on the top XGBoost features between the training and test windows. In particular, the test-set distribution of `counterpartyid_te` — does it have meaningful coverage on counterparties seen during training? — would help separate the question of whether the gap is driven by counterparty turnover from the question of whether it is driven by transaction-feature drift more generally.

### 6.2.3 Why HeteroGAT catches different fraud

The third interpretation concerns the divergence between metric performance and operational cost performance that emerges most clearly in the HeteroGAT result. HeteroGAT reports a test PR-AUC of 0.17 against XGBoost's 0.64, and a recall at its optimal threshold of 38% against XGBoost's 50%. By the metrics that the research question is framed around, HeteroGAT is the worse model. By the operational metric of fraud lost — the total monetary value of fraud the model failed to flag across the test window at the chosen operating threshold — HeteroGAT loses €2.05M against XGBoost's €2.92M. By this measure HeteroGAT is the better model, and the gap is wide.

The reconciliation is that the two models are not catching the same fraud. A model with higher recall and lower fraud lost is catching fraud at all value levels; a model with lower recall and lower fraud lost is selectively catching high-value fraud. The threshold-table evidence in Section 5.4 supports the second reading for HeteroGAT: at the threshold of 0.50, HeteroGAT loses only €135k, the lowest of any model at any threshold considered. At conservative thresholds (0.91 in the headline result), it still loses substantially less than XGBoost, despite catching fewer fraudulent transactions overall. The model's score distribution is not pushing high-value fraud into the high-recall tail; it is pushing it into the high-precision head.

The architectural explanation that makes this pattern coherent is that the structural signal HeteroGAT relies on is correlated with fraud value. Coordinated fraud — multiple compromised victims funnelling money to a small set of receiving accounts — produces a graph structure in which a single counterparty appears repeatedly as the receiver of suspicious flows from many different senders. This is precisely the kind of pattern that two-layer message passing should surface, because the receiving node aggregates messages from a high-fan-in neighbourhood that XGBoost, with no graph access, cannot see directly. Fraud campaigns with this structure are also disproportionately associated with the higher-value end of the loss distribution, both because criminal organisations operating coordinated victim-funnelling pipelines tend to direct higher-value individual transfers, and because the social-engineering scams that increasingly dominate Danish retail fraud (Brock, 2024; Europol, 2024) often involve guided large-amount transfers rather than small skimming attempts. If this reading is correct, HeteroGAT's lower recall is not a defect; it is a consequence of the model's score being driven by a structural feature that fires preferentially on high-value, high-coordination fraud.

The interpretation has direct implications for how the comparison should be read in light of the research question. The question asks whether heterogeneous graph structure improves fraud detection over a tabular baseline. The answer at the level of headline ranking metrics is no. The answer at the level of operational cost is more nuanced: the GNN-based detector — at least the HeteroGAT instance — appears to capture a structurally distinct slice of fraud, which on this dataset is the more financially consequential slice. The two answers are not contradictory; they reflect the two different things that "fraud detection performance" can mean in practice. A bank's transaction monitoring pipeline does not optimise PR-AUC; it optimises a downstream loss function that weighs recall, false-positive workload, and monetary loss against each other in proportions that reflect the bank's risk appetite, the regulatory environment, and the operational capacity of the SARO investigation team (Höppner et al., 2020). The GNN-based detector, on this evidence, has a different operating profile from the tabular one, and the question of which is preferable cannot be settled by PR-AUC alone.

> **Analytical gap to address before this section can stand:** the claim that HeteroGAT catches structurally distinct fraud — and specifically high-value fraud — needs explicit overlap analysis. The required figures: (i) Jaccard overlap of true-positive sets between XGBoost and HeteroGAT at matched flag-rate thresholds; (ii) value-distribution comparison of HeteroGAT-only true positives versus XGBoost-only true positives versus jointly-caught fraud; (iii) value-stratified PR-AUC, ideally per decile of transaction value, to show whether HeteroGAT's relative performance improves at the upper tail. Without (ii) in particular, the "high-value coordination" interpretation is plausible but unsubstantiated.

## 6.3 Generalisation of findings

The interpretation in Section 6.2 has so far stayed close to the empirical material. The discussion now broadens. Two threads run through the generalisation analysis. The first concerns the role of graph representation itself in an industry where most production pipelines remain resolutely tabular. The second concerns class imbalance, where the methodological choices made in this thesis sit within an unsettled debate about how rare-event detection should be trained.

### 6.3.1 Graph representation in financial crime

Outside of a small set of large institutions, transaction data in retail banking is rarely represented in graph format at all. Pipelines, feature stores, and monitoring systems are built around the row-per-transaction assumption that XGBoost embodies (Jullum et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2024), and the engineering effort required to maintain a graph alongside the existing tabular feature store is substantial. This raises a practical question that the empirical literature has so far underweighted: is graph structure worth the operational cost it imposes, given that strong tabular models already incorporate considerable relational information in the form of velocity aggregations, target encodings, and counterparty risk scores?

The findings of this thesis suggest that the answer depends on how one frames the comparison. Read as a pure performance comparison on the headline ranking metric, the result is consistent with the operational intuition that motivated the question in the first place: a tuned XGBoost with engineered features outperforms three heterogeneous GNN architectures on PR-AUC. This finding sits in tension with the broader empirical literature, which reports that heterogeneous GNNs improve over tabular baselines on financial crime tasks (Hu et al., 2020; Rao et al., 2022; Johannessen & Jullum, 2023; Singh et al., 2024), but the tension is more apparent than real once the structural difference between fraud detection and anti-money laundering is taken into account.

Anti-money laundering and transactional fraud detection are usually grouped together under the umbrella of financial crime detection, but they pose structurally different machine learning problems, and the asymmetry matters for how graph methods are likely to perform. In anti-money laundering, the transaction features are, by design of the launderer, unremarkable. The art of layering is precisely to disguise illicit flows as ordinary commercial activity, and the transaction-level features available to a tabular classifier carry comparatively little signal. The signal lives instead in the multi-hop structure of the flow, in the timing and fan-in fan-out patterns, and in the topological footprint of coordinated activity (Kurshan & Shen, 2021; Cardoso et al., 2022; Shwetha et al., 2025). For this class of problem, a graph representation is not merely a useful augmentation; it is, in many cases, the only place where the signal exists at all. Johannessen and Jullum's (2023) HMPNN result on DNB data, in which a heterogeneous GNN substantially outperformed both XGBoost and a homogeneous baseline, is consistent with this reading.

Transactional fraud, by contrast, is typified by features that are themselves anomalous. A fraudulent payment is often unusual in amount, in destination, in channel, in timing relative to the customer's usual behaviour, or in the novelty of the receiving account. These properties are precisely what velocity features and novelty flags capture, and the XGBoost feature importance analysis in Section 5.1.4 confirms that they dominate the model. The detective has, in effect, two shots at catching fraud: the transaction features and the graph. For anti-money laundering the detective has, in many cases, only the graph. The implication for the present study is not that graph structure is uninformative for fraud, but that its marginal informativeness over a strong tabular model is smaller than its marginal informativeness over the same baseline on AML, because much of what the graph would contribute is already captured, in feature-engineered form, by the tabular model. In a setting in which the tabular feature set were stripped down to raw transaction properties, the comparison would, on this reading, look quite different.

This framing strengthens rather than weakens the argument for evaluating GNNs on fraud data. The case for the graph is not that it must outperform a strong tabular model on aggregate metrics in order to be useful, but that it provides a complementary view of risk. The operational analysis interpreted in Section 6.2.3 supports this reading: HeteroGAT loses the smallest amount of money to undetected fraud across the test window, despite catching fewer fraudulent transactions overall than XGBoost. This is consistent with the broader pattern observed in deployed graph fraud detectors that combine GNN scoring with tabular triage rather than treating them as competing alternatives (Eddin et al., 2021; Rao et al., 2022). The deployment question, in this framing, is not "GNN or XGBoost" but "what role does each play in a layered detection stack", and the present study's findings are consistent with the literature pattern in which GNNs handle the structural cases that tabular models miss, while tabular models retain primary responsibility for the high-volume, feature-anomaly cases that they handle well.

### 6.3.2 Class imbalance

The second generalisation concerns the methodological treatment of class imbalance, where the choices made in this thesis sit within a real and unsettled debate in the fraud detection literature. The fraud rate in the experimental dataset is approximately one percent, which is high by the standards of confirmed fraud labelling but extreme by the standards of standard supervised classification benchmarks. The reviewed literature offers three broad responses to this kind of imbalance, and the choices among them have real consequences for what the headline metrics in Chapter 5 mean.

The first response is to retain the natural class distribution and rely on threshold-free metrics like PR-AUC, supplemented by cost-sensitive loss weighting at training time. This is the path taken in this thesis and in much of the recent AML literature (Johannessen & Jullum, 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). The argument in its favour is principally about calibration: a model trained on the true prior probability of fraud will produce score distributions whose top tail can be interpreted as estimated fraud probability, which in turn allows the operating threshold to be tuned to a desired precision-recall tradeoff at deployment without further recalibration. The argument against it is that with a 99-to-1 negative-to-positive ratio, the gradient signal from the minority class is quantitatively dominated by the majority class, and the model can converge to a near-trivial solution that captures only the most flagrant fraud (Cheng et al., 2024). Inverse-frequency loss weighting, used in this thesis, partially addresses this by amplifying the gradient contribution of the minority class, but it does not change the structural fact that the model sees fewer than ten thousand fraud examples in training.

The second response is to resample the training data toward a more balanced distribution, either by oversampling fraud, undersampling non-fraud, or generating synthetic fraud examples through methods such as SMOTE. This is the path taken in much of the older fraud detection literature and in some applied work on credit card fraud. Its principal disadvantage in the GNN context is that the graph structure is corrupted by resampling: oversampling fraud creates duplicate edges with identical neighbourhoods, which provides no additional structural information and can encourage the model to memorise specific patterns rather than learn generalisable structural signatures (Singh et al., 2024). Undersampling non-fraud, in turn, removes nodes and edges from the graph and so changes the very topology over which message passing aggregates, with the effect that the model is trained on a graph that is structurally different from the one it will be evaluated on. Synthetic fraud generation through SMOTE-like methods sits awkwardly with graph data because the synthesised examples lack a meaningful position in the network, and the literature has begun to explore graph-aware variants such as GraphSMOTE, but their adoption in financial crime applications remains limited.

The third response is to sidestep the explicit labels altogether through self-supervised representation learning, training on the structure of normal behaviour and treating fraud as deviation from this learned distribution. Cardoso et al. (2022) operationalise this strategy in LaundroGraph, which uses a self-supervised customer-to-transaction link prediction objective and reports a substantial improvement over a non-graph multilayer perceptron on a real bank's AML data. Singh et al. (2024) take a similar path with a heterogeneous graph variational autoencoder for credit card fraud, arguing that learning the latent distribution of legitimate transactions allows the model to flag anomalies without requiring a balanced fraud label set. The advantage of this family of approaches is that it converts an extreme class imbalance problem into an unsupervised representation learning problem, in which the abundance of normal transactions becomes an asset rather than a liability. The disadvantage, as Eddin et al. (2021) and Verlaan (2024) note, is that anomaly detection objectives can flag legitimate but unusual behaviour as fraud, inflating false positives in production. Self-supervised pretraining was discussed in the related work in Section 3.7 but was not part of the controlled experimental scope of this thesis, and the decision to evaluate end-to-end supervised models was made deliberately in order to keep the comparison interpretable.

What this thesis can contribute to the imbalance debate is a single empirical data point on a specific configuration: end-to-end supervised heterogeneous GNNs, trained with inverse-frequency loss weighting on the natural class distribution, evaluated on PR-AUC. The result is consistent with the broader pattern in the literature that this configuration is a viable but not dominant strategy on transaction data with strong tabular features, and the substantial validation-to-test PR-AUC drop interpreted in Section 6.2.2 suggests that the inverse-frequency weighting may be insufficient to stabilise the minority-class gradient against the temporal distributional shift exhibited by the dataset. Whether more aggressive cost weighting, or a hybrid approach that combines self-supervised pretraining on the abundance of normal transactions with end-to-end supervised fine-tuning on the smaller set of confirmed fraud labels, would close the gap between the GNN tracks and the tabular baseline is an empirical question that the present design cannot answer.

## 6.4 Interpretability and regulatory considerations

Cheng et al. (2024) catalogue interpretability under KYC regulation as one of the open problems on which graph-based fraud detection research must make further progress before deployment in production banking systems is realistic. The framing is apposite for this thesis, because it reframes the comparison between XGBoost and the heterogeneous GNNs in a way that the headline metrics do not capture: the two model families differ not only in detection performance but in how readily their decisions can be explained to the audiences who must act on them. Any model embedded in a transaction monitoring pipeline must justify its outputs to multiple stakeholders simultaneously, and the explanatory affordances of tabular and graph models are structurally different.

Gerlings and Constantiou (2023) document, in a qualitative study of a large European bank, that explainability requirements in transaction monitoring are stakeholder-specific rather than universal. A frontline investigator handling a flagged alert needs an explanation that surfaces the local features and relational context responsible for the flag, in a form that supports rapid disposition and onward escalation. A compliance officer auditing the bank's monitoring practices needs assurance that the model's behaviour is consistent with stated policy and that systematic biases or blind spots can be identified and remediated. A regulator assessing the institution's overall control environment needs a model whose decisions are reproducible, auditable, and explicable in the language of the relevant legislation. The customer whose transaction is blocked, finally, has an increasing legal entitlement to a meaningful explanation of the decision under both PSD2's liability regime and the broader regulatory direction set by the EU AI Act and the General Data Protection Regulation's right-to-explanation provisions. These four audiences impose four overlapping but not identical explainability requirements, and a model that satisfies one need not satisfy the others. Gerlings (2025) develops this multi-stakeholder framing at length in her doctoral work on xAI in high-risk decisions and argues that the design of the explanation interface is as consequential as the design of the underlying model.

The XGBoost baseline has the advantage, in this context, of mature post-hoc explanation tooling. SHAP values decompose individual predictions into per-feature contributions in a way that maps cleanly onto the engineered feature set used in the bank's existing pipelines, and feature importance summaries at the model level support the kinds of audit and bias assessments that compliance and regulator audiences require. The interpretability is not free; SHAP values for a tree ensemble of the size used in this thesis are computationally non-trivial at scale, and the explanations are local to the feature space rather than to the underlying business reality. But the path from a tree ensemble to a regulator-ready explanation is well-trodden in financial services and is one of the reasons that gradient-boosted decision trees remain the dominant baseline despite the theoretical attractions of more expressive models (Cheng et al., 2024; Jullum et al., 2020).

Heterogeneous graph neural networks pose a structurally harder interpretability problem. An explanation of a GNN's prediction for a specific transaction edge must, in principle, account for the contributions of the source and destination node embeddings, the edge features, and the messages received by each node from its neighbourhood across each layer of message passing. The relevant explanatory unit is not a feature vector but a subgraph, and the tooling for producing subgraph-level explanations remains substantially less mature than the tooling for feature-level explanations of tabular models. Rao et al. (2022) address this directly in xFraud by pairing a heterogeneous GNN detector with a separately trained post-hoc explainer that produces edge- and node-level attribution masks for each prediction. The two-component design is, in their telling, a deliberate response to the regulatory and operational reality of deploying graph models in financial settings: the detector handles the prediction problem, and the explainer handles the audit problem. This decoupling is one of the more practically important architectural patterns in the deployed graph fraud literature, and any future deployment of the heterogeneous architectures evaluated in this thesis would benefit from an analogous explainer module.

The regulatory backdrop adds further weight to the interpretability question. PSD2's Article 73 requires that banks provide written grounds for treating a transaction as suspected fraud when withholding a refund (European Parliament, 2015), which in practice imposes a documentation burden that any deployed model must support. The EU AI Act, adopted in 2024, classifies financial fraud detection systems in retail banking as high-risk applications and imposes additional requirements on transparency, human oversight, and the documentation of training data and model behaviour. The Danish Financial Supervisory Authority's enforcement priorities, informed in part by the Danske Bank Estonia case (Danske Bank, 2022; KirchMaier & Bjerregaard, 2019), place particular emphasis on the auditability of the institution's overall financial crime control environment. Each of these regulatory pressures converges on a common practical demand: that any model whose outputs trigger consequential actions on customer transactions must be capable of supporting an explanation that satisfies investigators, compliance officers, regulators, and, increasingly, affected customers themselves.

Gerlings et al. (2021) review the literature on the need for explainable AI and observe that the relative weight assigned to explainability versus predictive performance is itself a stakeholder-specific question. A model that achieves a one-percentage-point improvement in PR-AUC at the cost of a substantially harder explanation problem may be operationally worse than a model with a smaller PR-AUC but richer explanation tooling, depending on how the bank's decision processes around alert disposition are structured. This is one of the reasons why the empirical finding of this thesis, that XGBoost outperforms the heterogeneous GNNs on the headline metric, should not be read as definitively settling the question of which architecture family the bank should adopt. A graph-based detector with a slightly weaker headline metric but the structural capacity to surface neighbourhood-level explanations of why a specific transaction was flagged might be more valuable to investigators than a tabular model whose explanations are restricted to the feature space, and the comparative valuation is a function of the bank's investigation workflow rather than of the model's loss function.

This would not be a full xFraud-style explainer, but it would convert a theoretical claim about graph interpretability into a concrete demonstration of what the architectures evaluated in this thesis can already produce.

## 6.5 Operational viability

The interpretability question shades naturally into the broader question of operational viability, which Cheng et al. (2024) identify as the principal weakness of the graph-based fraud detection literature. They observe that the field's published performance gains have not, to date, translated into substantial production deployments in retail banking, and that the gap between research-grade demonstrations and operational systems is wider for graph-based methods than for tabular ones. The empirical work in this thesis was scoped to research-grade evaluation rather than to a deployable pipeline, but the question of what would have to be true for the heterogeneous GNNs evaluated here to be operationally viable is itself part of the discussion.

The first operational constraint is inference latency. Production fraud detection at Danske Bank operates within a latency envelope of approximately one hundred milliseconds per scoring decision, drawn from internal communication on operationally relevant inference budgets. The XGBoost baseline meets this comfortably; a tuned tree ensemble of the size used in this thesis scores a single transaction in under a millisecond on standard production hardware. The heterogeneous GNNs, by contrast, require a neighbourhood lookup at inference time. To score a single transaction edge, the model must retrieve the source and destination node embeddings, which in turn depend on the edge's two-hop neighbourhood. A naive implementation that recomputes the relevant subgraph at each inference call adds substantial latency relative to the tabular baseline, and the cost grows with neighbourhood density. In practice, deployed graph models address this through a combination of pre-computed node embeddings, neighbourhood sampling, and asynchronous embedding updates, but each of these introduces its own trade-off between embedding freshness and inference cost (Rao et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024). The headline result of this thesis does not include latency, and it is not safe to assume that the GNN architectures evaluated here would meet the production envelope without engineering work that the thesis did not undertake.

The second operational constraint is throughput. Eddin et al. (2021) note that financial institutions routinely process millions of events per day, and the fraud detection layer must scale to this volume without becoming a bottleneck. XGBoost throughput on commodity hardware is comfortably in the tens of thousands of transactions per second per core, and a horizontally scaled deployment can absorb the bank's full transaction volume with substantial headroom. Heterogeneous GNN throughput is harder to characterise without a specific deployment design, because it is dominated by the cost of subgraph construction rather than by the cost of the forward pass. The literature on production graph systems, principally driven by recommendation and search applications at large technology firms, has converged on a small set of patterns for high-throughput graph inference, but these patterns are not yet standard in financial services and their adoption would require investment in graph infrastructure that does not currently exist in most retail banks.

The third operational constraint is model lifecycle. Production fraud detectors are retrained on a cadence ranging from daily for fast-moving fraud typologies to monthly for more stable patterns, and the retraining pipeline must coexist with the inference pipeline without disrupting either. Tabular models like XGBoost have well-understood retraining workflows, with feature stores, model registries, and shadow-deployment patterns that the financial services industry has built up over the past decade. Graph-based retraining is less mature operationally. The graph itself must be incrementally updated as new transactions arrive, and the embeddings produced by an earlier version of the model are not, in general, comparable to embeddings produced by a retrained version, which means that the entire downstream scoring pipeline must be aware of model version transitions in a way that tabular pipelines need not be. Deprez et al. (2024), in their review of continual graph learning for AML systems, frame this as one of the principal open problems in operationalising graph methods for financial crime, and propose continual learning approaches that allow a deployed graph model to adapt incrementally rather than be retrained from scratch. None of these approaches has yet been validated at the scale of a major retail bank, and the operational pipeline implied by the graph-based track of this thesis would require substantial investment in continual-learning infrastructure to be viable.

The fourth and arguably most consequential operational consideration is concept drift. The temporal generalisation gap interpreted in Section 6.2.2 is, in part, a function of the fraud landscape itself shifting between the training window and the test window. Fraud is adversarial; criminals adapt to detection mechanisms, and the typologies that dominate the test window are not the same typologies that dominated the training window (Vallarino, 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). A production model that does not retrain frequently enough degrades, and the question of how frequently is itself an empirical question that depends on the rate at which the underlying fraud distribution moves. The thesis cannot speak directly to this question because it evaluates a single train-validate-test split, but the magnitude of the temporal gap reported in Chapter 5, particularly for HGT, is a quantitative warning that graph-based architectures may be more sensitive to concept drift than tabular ones. This is an architectural property worth investigating in its own right, and it sits alongside latency, throughput, and lifecycle as one of the four operational dimensions on which any deployment decision would have to be evaluated.

Taken together, these considerations support Cheng et al.'s (2024) characterisation of the graph fraud detection field as poorly operationalised. The empirical findings of this thesis are consistent with that characterisation: even setting aside the headline metric gap, the path from a research-grade GNN evaluation to a production-grade fraud detection deployment is meaningfully longer than the path from a research-grade XGBoost evaluation, and the bank would need to invest substantially in graph infrastructure, continual learning, and explanation tooling to make a heterogeneous GNN track operationally competitive with the tabular baseline. That investment may, on balance, be worth making, particularly in light of the operational cost finding interpreted in Section 6.2.3 — that GNN-based scoring captures fraud XGBoost does not — but the case for it must be made on those terms rather than on the basis of the headline metric alone.

> **Analytical gap to address before this section can stand:** the section makes claims about the relative latency of XGBoost and the heterogeneous GNNs without measured numbers. Even rough wall-clock estimates of single-edge scoring time for the trained models, on the same hardware, would substantially strengthen the operational viability argument and convert the latency claim from theoretical to empirical.

## 6.6 Limitations

The empirical comparison reported in Chapter 5 carries with it a set of structural limitations that bound what its findings should be taken to imply. Cheng et al. (2024), in the review already drawn on at several points, catalogue a set of open problems in graph-based fraud detection — interpretability under KYC regulation, scalability to real-time streams, temporal dynamics, label scarcity, and the accommodation of heterogeneous structures — that map directly onto the present study and into which the limitations discussed below all fall.

The first and most consequential limitation is the unidirectional scope of the dataset. The graph constructed in this thesis contains exclusively outgoing payments initiated by Danske Bank account holders. External counterparty nodes therefore appear only as receivers and never as senders, leaving the entirety of their outgoing behaviour unobservable. The structural signal available for external nodes is consequently restricted to the messages they receive from internal accounts, with no possibility of recovering a meaningful representation of how these counterparties themselves transact. In practice, this means that fraud patterns whose decisive structural signature lies in the receiving account's broader activity, such as a counterparty that aggregates funds from many compromised victims and rapidly disperses them onward, are invisible by construction. The same limitation does not apply to a bilateral transaction dataset covering both inbound and outbound flows, in which external nodes are first-class participants with observable two-sided behaviour. The Open Academic Graph that Hu et al. (2020) used to validate HGT, the eBay logs studied by Rao et al. (2022) in xFraud, and the proprietary DNB graph examined by Johannessen and Jullum (2023) all contain bidirectional structure, and the comparison reported here would benefit from being replicated on a similarly bilateral dataset.

The second limitation is that the empirical claim is anchored in a single institution. Danske Bank is a large Danish retail and commercial bank with a particular customer demographic, channel mix, and transaction profile, and the fraud typologies it encounters reflect that profile — domestically dominant, retail-heavy, and increasingly shaped by the social-engineering scams that have come to dominate the Danish payment landscape (Brock, 2024; Europol, 2024). The architectural findings of the cross-model comparison, namely that HGT generalises poorly under counterparty turnover, that HeteroGAT is the most temporally robust of the three GNNs, and that HMPNN under-converges on the available compute budget, are likely more transferable than the absolute performance numbers, because they reflect properties of the architectures rather than properties of the dataset. But even the architectural findings are conditional on the specific graph topology used in this thesis, and replication on data from a structurally different institution — a corporate bank with concentrated counterparty graphs, a payments-only fintech, or a bank with substantially different customer demographics — would be required to make the findings more broadly generalisable. The literature has so far underweighted the question of how transferable graph-based fraud findings are between institutions; Motie and Raahemi (2024) flag heavy reuse of a narrow benchmark set as a methodological weakness in their systematic review, and the present study does not escape this constraint.

The third limitation is the depth of the message passing stack. All three GNN architectures use two convolution layers, restricting neighbourhood aggregation to a two-hop radius around each transaction edge. This choice was justified in Section 4.5.4 by reference to over-smoothing (Hamilton, 2020; Cheng et al., 2024) and by the exponential memory cost of expanding receptive fields over dense financial graphs (Johannessen & Jullum, 2023; Rao et al., 2022). The substantive consequence for the discussion is that the comparison tests whether local relational context, broadly the immediate counterparties of a transaction's sender and receiver, improves detection over the tabular baseline. It does not test whether long-range graph structure, of the kind associated with multi-hop layering typologies in the AML literature (Kurshan & Shen, 2021; Cardoso et al., 2022), carries signal. Any conclusion about the value of heterogeneous graph representation should therefore be read as conditional on the two-hop receptive field; deeper architectures might recover the kinds of structural signatures this thesis is structurally unable to surface.

The fourth limitation is the temporal scope of the data. The training window covers roughly nine months, the validation window three, and the test window four, for a total of approximately sixteen months of activity. This is sufficient to expose the temporal generalisation gap interpreted in Section 6.2.2, but it is not sufficient to characterise the long-run distributional drift to which production fraud systems are subject. Production retraining cadences at large banks are typically measured in weeks rather than months, and a sixteen-month evaluation window does not provide enough seasonality to assess how the GNNs would behave under repeated retraining or under the kinds of regime shifts associated with fraud campaigns, regulatory changes, or shifts in customer behaviour. Deprez et al. (2024) review continual graph learning for anti-money laundering systems and emphasise that label arrival is delayed and that the underlying relational structure of fraud co-evolves with detection; the present study, with its single train-validate-test split, captures none of this dynamic.

A final note on labels is in order. The thesis treats the bank's confirmed-fraud labels as ground truth, but the production fraud labelling process is itself imperfect. False negatives — fraud that occurred but was not reported by the customer or detected by downstream investigation — are systematically absent from the label set, and this absence biases the supervised training of every model evaluated here. The bias is not architecture-specific, and to first order it should affect XGBoost and the heterogeneous GNNs comparably. But to second order, graph-based methods are arguably more sensitive to label noise because the noise propagates through neighbourhoods rather than remaining local to individual transactions, and a missed fraud label affects not only the score of the unflagged transaction but the embeddings of every node involved in its neighbourhood. The label limitation therefore deserves explicit acknowledgment as a constraint on the precision of the architectural comparison, not only on its absolute level.

### 6.6.1 Computational Constraints

A second cluster of limitations is operational rather than methodological. Danske Bank's internal infrastructure does not provide GPU compute for thesis-scale experimentation, constraining all model training to CPU. Eddin et al. (2021) observe that scalability issues arise routinely for financial institutions, which often process millions of events per day, rendering the use of entire transaction histories unfeasible without dedicated infrastructure; the constraint encountered in this thesis is a concrete instance of the same structural problem.

All three GNN architectures use two convolution layers, restricting neighbourhood aggregation to a two-hop radius around each transaction edge — a choice justified in Section 4.5.4 by reference to over-smoothing (Hamilton, 2020; Cheng et al., 2024) and by the exponential memory cost of expanding receptive fields over dense financial graphs (Johannessen & Jullum, 2023; Rao et al., 2022). The comparison in Chapter 5 consequently tests whether local relational context — broadly the immediate counterparties of a transaction's sender and receiver — improves detection over the tabular baseline. It does not test whether long-range graph structure, of the kind associated with multi-hop layering typologies in the AML literature (Kurshan & Shen, 2021; Cardoso et al., 2022), carries signal; deeper architectures, or mini-batch training methods that would allow scaling message passing beyond what fits in CPU memory, might recover structural signatures that this thesis is unable to surface.

The CPU constraint also meant that hyperparameter search for the GNN architectures was substantially less exhaustive than the fifty-trial Bayesian optimisation used for the XGBoost baseline. The GNN configurations reported in Chapter 5 are the result of more limited manual tuning over a narrower hyperparameter space. There is no principled reason to assume that the configurations identified are globally optimal for the heterogeneous architectures, and the literature suggests that GNN performance is meaningfully sensitive to choices around hidden dimension, number of attention heads, dropout, and learning rate schedule (Hu et al., 2020; Veličković et al., 2018). The HGT result, in particular, deserves to be read with this caveat in mind. Its near-collapse on the test set despite reasonable validation performance is structurally informative about temporal generalisation, but a more thorough hyperparameter search might recover stronger absolute performance and so change the magnitude, though not the direction, of the cross-architecture comparison.

A further computational consideration concerns reproducibility and model lifecycle. The graph cache approach used in this thesis writes the assembled HeteroData object to disk and reads it back at training time. This is appropriate for thesis-scale experimentation but does not constitute a production-grade pipeline. In a deployed setting, the graph would need to be incrementally updated as new transactions arrive, neighbourhood subgraphs would need to be sampled at inference time within the bank's latency envelope, and the entire feature pipeline would need to coexist with the existing tabular feature store. None of this was within scope for the thesis, but the absence of a deployable pipeline is itself a constraint on what the empirical results can be taken to imply about operational viability, as already discussed in Section 6.5.

## 6.7 Future work

The findings of this thesis open a number of directions in which the work most naturally extends. They fall into three rough clusters: extensions to the data, extensions to the model, and extensions to the evaluation. None of the directions outlined below was within scope for the thesis, but each follows directly from a specific limitation or open question identified in the preceding sections.

The most immediately useful extension on the data side is the addition of bidirectional flows. The unidirectional scope of the dataset, discussed in Section 6.6, is the single most consequential structural limit on what the experiments can reveal about graph-based fraud detection. A bilateral graph in which external counterparties appear as both senders and receivers would, in particular, allow the model to learn representations of counterparty-level behaviour that the present design is structurally unable to recover. Whether such a dataset is constructible at Danske Bank depends on data-sharing arrangements between Danske Bank and other institutions in the Danish payment system; in jurisdictions where central payment infrastructure is more transparent, this may be more readily achievable. A second, simpler data extension is the introduction of customer nodes alongside the existing account nodes. Customer-level KYC data, including age band, customer segment, customer-level risk rating, and onboarding date, would allow the graph to express customer-level risk explicitly rather than requiring it to be inferred from the aggregate behaviour of accounts owned by the same customer. The `owns` and `owned_by` edges between customer and account nodes mentioned in the methodology would, in this configuration, become first-class structural features of the graph rather than implementation details. Privacy and regulatory considerations would shape what KYC features can legitimately be incorporated; Paladugu (2025) discusses privacy-preserving graph embedding approaches that may be relevant.

A particularly promising data extension specific to fraud is the integration of OnCall signals. In recent fraud typologies, particularly the social-engineering scams that dominate the upper tail of loss in the Danish retail banking sector (Brock, 2024; Europol, 2024), the victim is typically being guided in real time by a malicious actor over the phone. The presence of an active call during the moments leading up to a transaction is one of the strongest known indicators that the transaction is fraudulent, and integrating call signals into the transaction graph as either an additional node type or as a temporal edge feature would convert a strong external signal into a graph-native feature. Whether the bank has access to call metadata in real time is an operational question rather than a methodological one, but the methodological extension is straightforward.

On the model side, the most natural extension is self-supervised pretraining. Section 3.7 covered LaundroGraph (Cardoso et al., 2022) and HGMAE (Tian et al., 2023) at length but explicitly excluded them from the controlled experimental scope. Their natural place in the present work would be as a pretraining stage that produces strong initial node embeddings on the basis of the abundance of normal transactions, followed by supervised fine-tuning on the smaller set of confirmed fraud labels. The expected benefit is twofold: the pretraining stage absorbs much of the structural signal of normal behaviour, allowing the supervised fine-tuning to focus on discriminating fraudulent edges from a normalised baseline; and the fine-tuning stage requires correspondingly less labelled fraud data, which addresses the label scarcity problem that Cheng et al. (2024) identify as one of the field's open problems. Whether self-supervised pretraining would close the gap between the GNN tracks and the tabular baseline observed in this thesis is an open empirical question, but the literature suggests that it is among the most likely candidates for doing so.

A second model-side extension is to deepen the message passing stack beyond the two layers used in the present experiments. The two-hop receptive field is sufficient to capture the kinds of local relational context that immediate counterparties contribute, but it is structurally unable to capture multi-hop laundering or fraud-laundering hybrid typologies that operate across longer paths in the graph. Deeper architectures bring their own difficulties, principally over-smoothing and exponentially growing memory requirements, but recent advances in over-smoothing mitigation, such as residual connections, jumping knowledge layers, and skip connections, may make four- or six-layer architectures tractable on graphs of the size used in this thesis with adequate compute (Hamilton, 2020).

A third model-side extension follows from the temporal generalisation gap interpreted in Section 6.2.2. Continual graph learning, as reviewed by Deprez et al. (2024), addresses the specific failure mode in which a graph model trained on an earlier window degrades sharply when deployed against a later one. The continual-learning extensions developed in that literature are designed precisely for the kind of regime the present thesis exhibits, and they would be a natural component of a deployment-grade extension of the work.

On the evaluation side, the most consequential extension is a full implementation of the instance-dependent cost-sensitive framework introduced by Höppner et al. (2020) and discussed but not implemented in Section 4.6. Recall, PR-AUC, and even the operational fraud-lost analysis in Section 5.4 all aggregate across transactions in ways that an instance-dependent cost framework would refuse to do. The framework's principal benefit is that it weighs each transaction by its specific monetary cost, in line with the PSD2 liability regime, rather than by uniform per-transaction cost. An evaluation conducted under a full instance-dependent cost-sensitive framework would also allow direct comparison of operating thresholds on monetary rather than statistical grounds, and would, in principle, shift the comparison between XGBoost and the heterogeneous GNNs in a direction that the headline PR-AUC metric does not capture, given the operational cost finding in Section 6.2.3.

A second evaluation extension is a controlled feature-parity study. The deliberate feature asymmetry interpreted in Section 6.2.1 was justified as the choice that makes the research question interpretable, but it leaves unanswered the related question of how the GNNs would perform if given access to the same feature set. A complementary study in which both tracks operate on identical feature sets, with the GNNs receiving velocity features either as additional edge features or as additional customer-node features, would isolate the marginal contribution of graph structure from the marginal contribution of feature engineering. The expected outcome is not obvious: if the velocity features carry signal that the graph cannot recover, parity should improve the GNNs more than XGBoost; if they do not, parity should leave the relative ordering largely unchanged.

A final evaluation extension concerns data quality. The thesis takes the bank's confirmed-fraud labels as ground truth, but the production fraud labelling process is itself imperfect, as noted in Section 6.6. Improvements in the upstream label-quality pipeline, including delayed-label validation, semi-supervised label refinement, and explicit modelling of the labelling process as a noisy oracle, would benefit every model in the comparison and would, in particular, benefit graph-based methods. Data quality, as the thesis has emphasised throughout, is the foundation on which both rules and models depend, and is a precondition rather than a candidate for replacement.

The directions outlined here are not exhaustive. The intersection of graph methods, financial crime detection, and operational deployment is among the most active areas in applied machine learning, and the literature is moving quickly. Within the scope of Danske Bank's specific operational and regulatory constraints, however, the extensions above are the ones that follow most directly from the empirical findings of this thesis, and the ones whose pursuit would most directly address the limitations under which those findings were produced.
